Impact Cases

Real-world impact of our research

ASICA: Achieving Self-directed Integrated Cancer Aftercare

Funded by Cancer Research UK (CRUK) · £794K

Challenge: Melanoma is now the second most common cancer in the UK among people under 50. After treatment, patients are advised to perform monthly total-skin self-examinations (TSSE), but adherence is poor. Patients often experience anxiety about recurrence and face long waits to access specialist clinical input for skin concerns — leading to either unnecessary hospital visits or delays in detecting new or recurrent melanomas.

Solution: ASICA is a digital tablet application that empowers melanoma survivors to self-monitor between clinical appointments. Patients perform guided skin self-examinations and submit photos of concerns directly to a specialist nurse via the app. Natural language generation (NLG) technology is used to communicate with patients in clear, reassuring language tailored to their individual situation — helping them understand their skin health without causing unnecessary alarm.

Impact:

  • 120 patients participated in the trial; over half proactively submitted skin concern information through the app.
  • In more than 80% of cases where patients reported a concern, the specialist nurse was able to provide reassurance remotely — avoiding unnecessary hospital visits.
  • Using the app over 12 months showed no increase in worry or anxiety and slight improvements in general quality of life.
  • The intervention was proven clinically feasible and integrated smoothly with routine follow-up care.

Reach: Building on the UK trial, the team extended ASICA internationally — piloting the system in the USA (CONVERGE award, $10,000) and assessing its potential in other countries through a dedicated international pilot (KEC Award, £8,000), demonstrating the scalability of the approach across different healthcare systems.

Key Collaborators: Prof. Peter Murchie (Primary Care), Prof. Ehud Reiter (NLG), Dr. Dewei Yi (AI/Computer Vision)

AI-Driven Safer Driving Feedback for Non-Western Countries

PhD Project · Iniakpokeikiye Peter Thompson

Challenge: Road traffic accidents kill significantly more people in Nigeria than in the UK, yet most research on AI-driven driving safety systems has focused exclusively on Western contexts, where road rules, driving culture, and infrastructure differ substantially.

Solution: Building on prior UK work using smartphone sensors and natural language generation (NLG) to deliver personalised driving feedback, this project adapted the approach for Nigeria — generating culturally-attuned safety feedback tailored to Nigerian road conditions and driver behaviour norms.

Impact: A real-world evaluation with Nigerian drivers demonstrated a 20% reduction in unsafe driving incidents. Post-PhD funding has been secured to enhance deployment, and collaboration has begun with partners in Cameroon — pointing towards broader regional applicability.

Key Collaborators: Dr. Dewei Yi, Prof. Ehud Reiter

Smartawl: AI-Powered Fish Detection and Size Estimation

Funded by Fisheries Innovation & Sustainability (FIS) · £408K (Smartawl 5.0) + £157K (Smartawl 4.0)

Challenge: Accurate fish detection and size estimation are critical for sustainable fisheries management, but traditional manual sampling methods are labour-intensive and do not scale to the volumes needed for robust stock assessment.

Solution: Smartawl developed computer vision methods — including curved ellipse modelling and voting-based matching — to automatically detect fish and estimate their size from camera footage, designed to integrate with commercial fishing operations without disrupting activities.

Impact: The technology provides fisheries scientists and regulators with a scalable, low-cost alternative to manual measurement, improving the quality and volume of data available for sustainable fisheries management in Scottish and UK waters.

Key Collaborators: Dr. Dewei Yi, Prof. Paul G. Fernandes (Heriot-Watt University), Dr. Yining Hua, Yiren Li
